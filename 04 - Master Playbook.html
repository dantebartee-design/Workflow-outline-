Section 1

Introduction & purpose

This playbook is the single source of truth for how we produce and file disclosures. It exists so that any disclosure — drafted by Dante, finished by Holly, or done start-to-finish by one person — comes out the same way: correctly named, in the right folder, with a clean revision trail, and ending as a finalised record in Clio.

Read it once end-to-end. After that, jump to the section you need: the ground rules when you're naming or filing; the workflow flowchart when you're unsure who does what next; the solo instructions when you're running a disclosure on your own.

The one principle behind all of it

The folder a document sits in tells you its stage; the initials in its filename tell you who has touched it. Keep those two things honest and the whole process stays legible — no version-number guessing, no lost drafts.

Section 2

Ground rules — naming & folders

File naming convention

20260601 initial disclosure statement (D L)
  • Date first — YYYYMMDD (here, 1 June 2026). Keeps files in chronological order automatically.
  • Plain description in lowercase — say what the document is, no codes.
  • Initials in parentheses — (D) = Dante drafted; (D L) = Dante drafted, Lawyer reviewed.
  • Add initials, don't replace — the trail grows left-to-right: (D L D L).
  • One document, one name — update the same file; never "final_final_v3".
  • No special characters beyond spaces and the parentheses.

How notes get added

Keep a one-line note with each change — a comment in the document or a line in a running log. The initials say who last touched it; the note says what changed. Together they are your complete revision history.

Section 5

Holly solo — front end, from scratch

When to use this

You're starting a brand-new disclosure yourself — no draft from Dante. You own the whole document, front to back.

  1. Open Claude Teams and start a new disclosure draft from the playbook / template.
  2. Name the file right away using the convention, with your initial: 20260601 initial disclosure statement (L)
  3. Save it into 📁 Shared / To Be Disclosed. This is its home while you write.
  4. Draft in Claude Teams, working from the template and the matter facts. Let Claude do the heavy lifting on structure and first language.
  5. Collect exhibits as you go — keep them together so they're ready to file.
  6. Self-review against the disclosure checklist (Section 7). Fix anything before it moves.
  7. Move the draft to 📁 Disclosures, and put every exhibit into 📁 Disclosures / Attachments.
  8. Do your approval pass. You're both author and approver here — keep your initial (L) as the sign-off on the trail.
  9. Move the finalised document to 📁 Done. Nothing changes after this.
  10. Export the final copy to Clio (system of record), then file / serve.
Section 6

Holly solo — back end, finishing a started draft

When to use this

Dante has already drafted the document and handed it over. You're picking it up to review, finish, and file.

  1. Find Dante's draft in 📁 Shared / To Be Disclosed — its name ends with (D).
  2. Open it in Claude Teams and read Dante's notes first — he'll have flagged gaps or questions for you.
  3. Review the draft against the matter and the checklist (Section 7).
  4. Make your edits in the same file, then append your initial: (D) → (D L)
  5. Add a short revision note for anything significant you changed or need confirmed.
  6. If you need Dante to action something, that's a handoff back — leave a note and let him pick it up (he'll append → (D L D)). Otherwise, carry on.
  7. Move it to 📁 Disclosures; exhibits to 📁 Disclosures / Attachments.
  8. Final approval pass — the trail ends on your initial, e.g. (D L).
  9. Move to 📁 Done, then export to Clio and file / serve.
Section 7

The disclosure checklist

When to use this

Run this at the self-review gate — before any document leaves “To Be Disclosed”. Tick every box; if one can't be ticked, it isn't ready. The process items (naming, folders, sign-off) are non-negotiable; the substantive items are prompts — final judgement on legal sufficiency always rests with the lawyer. A standalone fillable copy is available to print and keep at your desk.

1 Intake & setup
  • Matter open in Clio; client & matter confirmed.
  • Disclosure type and deadline identified.
  • All source documents gathered.
  • Working from the approved template / playbook.
2 Naming & filing
  • Named to convention: YYYYMMDD description (initials)
  • Date first; description plain lowercase.
  • Initials reflect who has touched it.
  • Saved in the correct folder for its stage.
  • One file — no duplicate versions.
3 Content & accuracy
  • All required sections present.
  • Names, dates & references correct.
  • Figures & facts verified against source.
  • Cross-references & exhibit labels match.
  • No placeholders or “TBC” left in.
  • Read end-to-end at least once.
4 Exhibits & attachments
  • Every referenced exhibit is attached.
  • Stored in 📁 Disclosures / Attachments.
  • Each exhibit labelled & dated.
  • No stray or privileged docs included.
5 Review & revision trail
  • A note recorded for each significant change.
  • Initials appended (not replaced).
  • Outstanding questions flagged or resolved.
  • Any handoff back is clearly noted.
6 Approval & filing
  • Lawyer review complete; approval in the filename.
  • No tracked changes / comments in the final.
  • Moved to 📁 Done — locked.
  • Final copy exported to Clio.
  • Filed / served by deadline; confirmation saved.
Section 8

Agentic workflows — down the road

The one rule

An agent doesn't invent the process — it executes the one you've already proven works. It's the automation version of this playbook, and it only arrives after the manual process is stable (roughly 2–3 disclosures in).

Now

Manual

Dante drafts, Holly approves — by hand. The job here is to prove and document the process until it stops changing.

Soon

Stable playbook

After a few disclosures, this document is solid and the steps don't shift between cases. That stability is the green light.

Later

Agent-assisted

An agent runs the routine spine; Holly keeps the judgement and the final approval.

What the agent will and won't do

The agent handles (routine)

Drafts the first version from the template · applies the naming convention automatically · files it into the correct folder · gathers exhibits into Attachments · flags it for Holly with notes. Essentially, everything in Dante's lane that's the same every time.

Holly keeps (judgement)

The review. The approval gate. Any case-specific call. Nothing is moved to Done, exported to Clio, or filed without her sign-off — the human is never removed from the disclosure handoff.

The direction is one-way

You move from a proven manual process, to a written playbook, to an agent — never the reverse. Skipping ahead just automates mistakes onto a process you haven't yet trusted.

Manual proof ──► Playbook ──► Agent

The agent lives alongside the team's other proven tools and executes this exact flowchart — the same lanes, the same naming convention, the same folder moves, the same approval gate. When it arrives, nothing about what good looks like changes; only who does the routine parts.
